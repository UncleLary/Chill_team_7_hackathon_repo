Snails are fascinating gastropod mollusks with unique biological adaptations. The radula is a ribbon-like feeding organ covered with rows of tiny teeth that snails use to scrape food from surfaces. This remarkable structure allows them to feed on algae, plant material, and detritus.

The mantle is specialized tissue that lines the shell and secretes the calcium carbonate material that forms the snail's protective shell. This shell grows throughout the snail's lifetime, expanding in a spiral pattern.

Regarding respiration, land snails utilize lungs to breathe air, while aquatic snails typically use gills. This adaptation allowed snails to colonize terrestrial environments.

Unlike vertebrates, snails lack bones and instead have a soft body protected primarily by their shell. Their skeleton is minimal, consisting mainly of cartilaginous structures in some species.

The muscular foot is a broad, flat muscle at the base of the snail's body used for locomotion and movement. By contracting muscles in the foot and secreting mucus, snails can glide across surfaces.
